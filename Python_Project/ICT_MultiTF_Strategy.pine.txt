1. Define Higher‑Timeframe Bias

	1. Choose HTF M15 as your **intention** timeframe. 
	2. Mark clear swing highs/lows and identify valid Break of Structure (BOS) and Change of Character (CHOCH).  
   		- BOS = continuation in the existing trend direction.  
   		- CHOCH = first structural break suggesting potential trend reversal. 
	3. Decide if market is overall bullish (HH–HL) or bearish (LL–LH). You only want to trade in line with this HTF order flow, unless you very 		   deliberately trade a pullback with pragmatic TP.

2. Map HTF Trading Range and Premium/Discount

	1. On the chosen HTF, mark the current trading range: last major swing low to last major swing high.
	2. Apply Fibonacci with levels 0 / 0.5 / 1 from low to high (or high to low if bearish) to separate:  
   		- Premium region (sell zones in a downtrend).  
   		- Discount region (buy zones in an uptrend).
	3. Only look for:  
   		- HTF supply zones inside premium for shorts.  
   		- HTF demand zones inside discount for longs.  
   	  *This filters out many weak zones and fake CHOCHs.

3. Identify HTF Points of Interest (POI)

	1. Within those premium/discount regions, mark **s	ply** and **demand** zones (order blocks):  
   		- The last opposing candle or consolidation that caused a strong impulse and structural break (BOS/CHOCH). 
	2. Include wicks: HTF wicks usually contain clear LTF zones (“hidden” supply/demand).
	3. Ensure each POI is causative: there must be a clear structural effect (BOS or CHOCH) originating from that zone. “Remember, there has to be a 	   cause.” 

4. Read Liquidity: “See the Liquidity or Be the Liquidity”

	1. Mark obvious liquidity pools:  
   		- Equal highs / equal lows.  
   		- Trendline touches (third touch, classic TL trading).  
   		- Range highs/lows (textbook support/resistance). 
	2. Understand these are fuel, not trade entries:  
   		- Retail is taught to sell double tops and buy double bottoms, but these levels are typically swept.  
   		- Trendlines and ranges gather liquidity that is later hunted
	3. Expect price to:  
   		- Move toward liquidity pools.  
   		- Sweep them and then reverse from your POI or LTF zone.

5. Wait for Liquidity Sweep into HTF POI

	1. Combine steps 2–4:  
   		- In a bearish bias, watch price push up into premium, taking out equal highs/trendline highs/range highs on the way to your HTF supply.  
   		- In a bullish bias, watch price push down into discount, taking out equal lows/trendline lows/range lows on the way to your HTF demand. 
	2. Ideal scenario (both directions):  
   		- Liquidity sweep (stop‑hunt) occurs right into or just through your HTF POI (supply/demand).  
   		- That sweep itself often forms your inducement for the reversal.

6. Drop to LTF M1: CHOCH in Opposite Direction

	1. Once price taps your HTF POI, **flip to lower timeframe** (e.g M1).
	2. On LTF, look for a CHOCH in the opposite direction of the prior LTF move:  
   		- For a short: inside HTF supply, wait for the LTF bullish structure (HH–HL) to break to the downside (first LH/LL).  
   		- For a long: inside HTF demand, wait for the LTF bearish structure (LL–LH) to break to the upside (first HL/HH).
	3. Ignore “false CHOCH” that does not come from a meaningful POI or fails to break meaningful structure; distinguish between false vs actual CHOCH  	   around traps.

7. Mark the LTF Execution Zone (Order Block / Mitigation)

	1. After the LTF CHOCH, identify the newly created LTF supply (for shorts) or demand (for longs) that **caused** that CHOCH or BOS.
	2. This LTF zone is your primary execution area:  
   		- It may be the last opposing candle before the CHOCH impulse.  
   		- It may be a “breaker” or “flip” zone where supply lost control and demand took over (or vice versa).
	3. Note prior mitigations:  
   		- check for chains of prior reactions “Demand Chain” etc., which help anticipate where price may react and refine POI selection.

8. Choose Entry Style: Risk vs Confirmation(for the entry i would to test each one for their own seperate way)

	Two entry styles, both built on the HTF POI + LTF CHOCH framework.
	8.1 Risk Entry
		1. Once HTF POI and LTF zone are identified, place a limit (or instant) order at the LTF zone.
		2. Conditions:  
   			- You do not wait for a second CHOCH or micro‑confirmation.  
   			- You accept lower win rate but better RR because entry is at or near the zone’s edge.
	8.2 Confirmation Entry
		1. Let price tap the LTF execution zone and then drop even lower (or stay on same LTF) and look for a further micro‑CHOCH / BOS in your 			   intended direction(M1).
		2. Enter after that local confirmation, often using one of the entry techniques:  
   			- Candle close / next candle open.  
   			- Break of the candle.  
   			- Limit at 50% of engulfing candle. 
		3. Higher probability but often less RR and more time‑consuming.

9. Buffer for Spread and Practical Execution
	1. Add spread to stops and entries so you do not miss fills by 0.5 pips, for example:  
   		- If spread = 0.5 pips, add 0.5 pips to both SL and entry (buys and sells). 
	2. If price does not fully mitigate the original HTF zone but gives valid signals, adapt:  
   		- Don’t get stuck waiting for a “perfect” touch if there is a new valid POI and CHOCH.  
   		- “No risk, no reward” and “be reactive, not predictive.” 

10. Stop‑Loss Placement
	1. For risk or confirmation entries:  
   		- Place SL beyond the structure/zone: above LTF supply for shorts, below LTF demand for longs. 
	2. Account for:  
   		- Spread buffering as above.  
   		- Possible wick sweeps beyond the body; using wicks is recognized as valid as long as the structure remains intact. 

11. Targets: Use HTF Structure, Ranges and Liquidity
	1. Choose targets aligned with HTF structure:  
   		- In a sell from HTF supply, typical targets are HTF lows of the range or the next clear discount zone.  
   		- In a buy from HTF demand, targets are HTF highs of the range or the next clear premium zone.
	2. Integrate liquidity:  
   		- Aim at liquidity pools (equal highs/lows, range boundaries, trendline extremes).  
   		- Many setups show “targeting the lows” or “targeting the highs” after traps and sweeps.

12. Flip Entries (Supply → Demand, Demand → Supply)
	1. A flip occurs when a supply zone is violated and demand takes control, or vice versa. 
	2. Conditions for a valid flip entry:  
   		- Must be in continuation of the current main trend, not counter‑trend.  
   		- Supply fails to hold and that failure itself creates a demand zone which then becomes your POI.
	3. Prefer entries after the CHOCH associated with the flip.

13. Managing Multiple Timeframes
	1. Core template: HTF intention + LTF execution:  
   		- M15 POI with M1 entries, targeting M15 lows, stops above M1 structure/zone. 
	2. When in doubt, zoom out:  
   		- A single H4 candle equals many LTF candles; always keep HTF structure in mind when trading LTF setups.

14. Valid vs Invalid Structure and No‑Trade Zones
	1. A BOS is only valid if price makes a **new** high or low beyond the previous swing. 
	2. Zones that do not lead to a new structural high/low are invalid as POIs.  
	3. Do not trade from highs/lows that are not structurally valid; explicitly label such areas as “No Trade” in examples. 

15. High‑Probability Entry Model Structure
	1. HTF bias and POI (premium for shorts, discount for longs).  
	2. Clear liquidity on the approach (trendlines, equal highs/lows, ranges).  
	3. Sweep of that liquidity into HTF POI.  
	4. LTF CHOCH in opposite direction.  
	5. LTF execution zone (order block/flip) that caused the CHOCH.  
	6. Risk or confirmation entry from that zone.  
	7. Targets at HTF structure/next liquidity pool.

16. Psychological and Plan Components (Non‑Technical but Part of the Strategy)
	1. Risk per trade: typically 1–3% or less.  
	2. Expectancy and journaling: track trades, review winners and losers, and calculate expectancy.  
	3. Mental preparation: sleep, emotional state, avoiding FOMO, accepting losses as inevitable.  
	4. Roadmap: learn basics → choose system → build plan → backtest → paper trade → go live → track/review.  
	These steps do not change the technical entry model but define how it should be implemented consistently. 

17. Complete Execution Checklist (Condensed)
For one trade, the exact ordered checklist implied by the book is:
	1. Select market and HTF (e.g.M15).  
	2. Determine HTF trend using valid BOS/CHOCH.  
	3. Mark HTF trading range and apply premium/discount.  
	4. Within correct region (premium or discount), mark HTF POI (supply/demand) with a clear structural “cause”.  
	5. Identify major liquidity on the path to that POI (equal highs/lows, trendlines, ranges).  
	6. Wait for price to move into the HTF POI, ideally sweeping nearby liquidity.  
	7. Once in HTF POI, drop to LTF(M1) and wait for CHOCH in the intended direction.  
	8. Mark the LTF execution zone (order block / flip / breaker that caused CHOCH).  
	9. Decide: risk entry (limit in zone) or confirmation entry (extra CHOCH/BOS on even lower TF or micro‑structure).  
	10. Set SL beyond the LTF zone/structure, adding spread buffer.  
	11. Set TP at logical HTF target: range high/low or next liquidity pool; ensure a favorable RR.  
	12. Execute according to the plan; do not move stops emotionally, adapt only if new valid structure forms.  
	13. After trade closes, journal: grade entry, note confluences (HTF POI, liquidity, CHOCH, imbalance, etc.), and update expectancy.
